# 2. Audiences & personas

← [Plan index](README.md)

The plan addresses five concrete personas. Every page should be readable as serving one or two of them; if it serves none, cut it.

## 2.1 Maya — the grad researcher

PhD candidate, year 3, sociology or public-health adjacent. Has 22 interview transcripts. Has been told by her advisor to "use NVivo" but watched the YouTube tutorial and felt nauseous. Budget: low; .edu email available.

- **Wants:** to finish coding by end of semester. Knows the words "thematic" and "grounded theory" but isn't a methodologist.
- **Fears:** picking a tool her advisor / committee will sneer at. Wasting a week learning software.
- **Signals she cares about:** how to cite the tool in her dissertation, whether her PI's lab will be able to read her project, whether export goes to NVivo's QDPX format.
- **Lands on:** `/` → `/methodology/thematic-analysis` → `/pricing` (sees .edu discount) → signup.

## 2.2 Dr. Chen — the lab PI / methods professor

Tenured, runs a research group of 4–8 students. Has been using NVivo or MAXQDA for 15 years and feels mildly imprisoned by it. Budget: department grant or seat-license.

- **Wants:** consistent codebook across his students, intercoder reliability for the methods paper, audit trail for IRB.
- **Fears:** asking the IT department to procure new software (knows what that looks like). Worse: training his lab on something that disappears in two years.
- **Signals he cares about:** intercoder κ, audit logs, sub-processors, DPA, longevity of vendor.
- **Lands on:** `/` → `/for-teams` → `/trust` → `/customers/[similar-lab]` → starts trial.

## 2.3 Dana — the IRB / research-administration officer

Reviews research protocols for human-subjects compliance. Doesn't do qualitative coding herself; vets the tools that touch participant data.

- **Wants:** plain-English answers to: where is data stored, who has access, can we delete on request, where is the DPA.
- **Fears:** signing off on a tool that gets named in a breach notice. _Also,_ signing off on a tool that quietly trains on participant transcripts.
- **Signals she cares about:** EU residency, sub-processor list, DPA availability, retention configurability, SOC 2 / ISO posture, **AI use policy**.
- **Lands on:** `/trust` → `/trust/ai` → `/for-institutions` → emails research desk.

## 2.4 Priya — the UX researcher

Senior researcher at a B2B SaaS. Already uses tools like Dovetail or Reduct. Considering QualCanvas for a longitudinal study where Dovetail's coding tools feel thin.

- **Wants:** real visual canvas, easy export for stakeholder readouts, fast onboarding for ICs.
- **Fears:** academic-flavored tools that don't speak product language. Tools that require training.
- **Signals she cares about:** speed of "highlight → code → show stakeholder," modern UX, integrations.
- **Lands on:** `/` → tries interactive demo → `/pricing` → signup.

## 2.5 Tom — the studio prospect

CTO or founder of a 5–25 person business. Saw QualCanvas mentioned somewhere; clicked the footer attribution; wants to know if the team behind it could build _his_ thing.

- **Wants:** evidence the studio can build to a craft tier.
- **Fears:** agencies that over-promise.
- **Signals he cares about:** how the site itself feels under his cursor. Lighthouse, animation polish, accessibility, attention to detail.
- **Lands on:** anywhere on qualcanvas.com → footer attribution → jmsdevlab.com.

**Implication:** Tom never sees a CTA aimed at him. He's served entirely by the site being good. That's the discipline we have to keep.

## 2.6 On persona overlap

A single visitor may be two personas at once — a methods professor (Dr. Chen) who also reviews vendors for the department (acts as Dana). A grad student (Maya) running her advisor's research group (acts as Dr. Chen). Pages must read coherently to combined readers. The test: a person who is _both_ Maya and Dr. Chen should never feel addressed by the wrong voice on the same page. This is what kills "growth-marketer" copy on academic sites — it speaks to one persona and alienates the rest.
